# The Turing Test: Can Machines Think?

In 1950, Alan Turing published a landmark paper in the journal *Mind* titled **"Computing Machinery and Intelligence"**. It opened with a deceptively simple question: *Can machines think?*

Rather than attempting to define "thinking" philosophically, Turing proposed a practical test — the **Imitation Game**. A human interrogator would converse via text with both a human and a machine. If the interrogator could not reliably distinguish the machine from the human, the machine could be said to exhibit intelligent behaviour.

## Why It Mattered

The Turing Test shifted the conversation from metaphysics to engineering. It asked not *what* intelligence is, but *what it looks like from the outside* — a pragmatic, behaviourist stance that proved enormously influential.

## Legacy

Decades later, the test remains controversial. Critics argue it measures deception rather than understanding. Proponents argue it sets a meaningful, empirical bar. Either way, it anchors every serious discussion of machine intelligence.
